GUI History

Jeremy Reimer

I didn't know much about the history of the GUI appart from rumours that said that Steve Jobs copied a bunch of dudes and became filthy rich. So I was glad to learn a bit about the history of the GUI.

As seems to be common place in these articles about computery matters, I noted that there is a parti pris as early as in the first page on the topic of whether or not people become less competant with computers ("He envisionned the computer not as a replacement for human intellect, about a tool for enhancing it"). To Reimer that's a silent yes it seems.

I thought the history of the developments of the GUI to be interesting, but what struck me the most was how many similarities there are between the "mother of all demo", as Reimer puts it, and modern hardware. A keyboard, a mouse, a camera and remote connection, hypertext.. It was surprising for me to realise how little has changed in all this time, especially in an industry that is apparently so fast-paced. When I look at my laptop, I know it's much smaller and faster than what they used at Englebart's demonstration, but to me the similarities stand out much more than the differences. I suppose my laptop has a different mind of mouse too. And capacity for programs that Englebart didn't have, like music programs, drawing programs, etc. It has a (broken) touch screen too.

I suppose there have been some major changes in the industry, like touch screens and phone sized computers. We can connect all kinds of different devices with a computer too. Still my first reaction was to think about how much computers didn't change.

Kay: Personal Dynamic Media

In a sense this is a nice extension to the History of the GUI article which talks about only very concrete applications of computers, and also ends its history with the mac OS X. I'm still impressed with how involved a printing company was with the creation of personal computers.
Printing started a revolution in the 15th c and so it would again in the 20th, huh! Also I noted in this article and the previous ones how early the importance of object oriented program was understood.

Reading about how the Dynabook could be hooked up to "organ-like keyboard for playing music" makes me want to try one of those so badly. I'm also kind of surprised that a printing company would venture into things that were not related to visual media.
I think this would be an indicator of some level of freedom in how the Dynabook's team was working which would probably please Roy Rosenzweig. I also gasped at the story of the 12 year old who wrote programs with Smalltalk and taught it to her friends.

"The imagination and boldness of the mid-1970s Dynabook vision" indeed! But to be honest that also brings me back to the idea that at the core computers and their applications haven't changed all the much despite their efficiency improving drastically.
