Turckle article
Video Games and Computer Hoding Power

I could appreciate a deeper look into the psychology of computer usage, finally, after all these comments about dehumanisation and loss of empathy woven into the last articles.
Also, while I would readily agree with the statement that computer holding power has absolutely nothing to do with video game narratives (in most cases), good narratives are needed for good visual arts, which I feel is an important factor in the appeal of a game. Though still, it could be argued that good visual arts also loop back into the point that video game holding power is somethin' else.

"more posessed by the game than playing it". I've always thought about video game addiction as being most likely a symptom of another problem, not the source of a problem in itself.

"a culture of rules and simulation"... where are we going with this? He talks about the process of learning how to automatize reflexes and movements in order to outdo oneself at a certain game, and relates it the piano, which I can totally relate to! On the piano however, repeating the same thing over and over for hours might be the process but oh boy is the feeling not at all the same - much more draining for the brain and easy to get distracted from.

Interestingly, I've had that same thought relating interaction with a computer, particularly but not limited to the context of video games, to a "conversation". To me, the computer is in fact a machine which responds to your input much like you would like a human to respond to input such as your questions, thoughts, musical riffs.. What I find is that a computer responds in a manner which one comes to anticipate and expect, and not be deceived by too often. Less often perhaps than nature which is full of unexpected events!
I've always understood that as being the more fulfilling aspect of computer interaction, that which makes users gravitate around their screen until 4am for no good reason. It reminds me quite vividly sometimes of being 13 years old and staying up as late as I could with good friends and not feeling the need to rest. Perhaps computers subtly bring you back to childhood..

I heard that's why cats like it when you pet them. Purr.

The author talks about enjoyement of highscores and feeling like a game adapts to your level of skill. He talks about kid Jarish's relationship with video games, and the contrast of euphoric and depressive feelings that surround it. He talks about his exaltation of the fantastic characters, and puts a good twist on the story when he talks about Jarish developping a passion for creative programming.

Finally, we get to the dangers of "infatuation with computer microworlds", like Narcissus and his reflection. "Most people don't become addicted to video games just as most people who diet don't become anorexic. But when they use these powerful mateirlas to measure themselves, they are at risk".
Sure! I can agree with both statements, I don't think video games are automatically a trap for your mental health, but having played and stopped playing games that can easily become a part of your ego (WoW for example), I feel like I've experienced both ends of that spectrum.
