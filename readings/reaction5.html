GNU manifesto

GNU manifesto (1985)

In the GNU Manifesto Richard Stallman describes his intentions in creating and distributing GNU, how he will make it, why and what kind of impact he wants his work to have on society. I found especially interesting the final few paragraphs of the text in which he describes possible directions in which he could see society taking.
He envisions a world on track for "postscarcity", where technology enables everyone to work as little as ten hours in a week (on delightful tasks such as robot repairs and asteroid prospecting). He places programming as a free creative passtime for all and gives the professional programmer a place within every business. That is the world which free access to technology would bring!

As I read this in 2018 it sounds kind of utopic. We are pretty darn far away from a world where tools or digital tools are freely accessible to everyone. I'm subscribed to Adobe CC for school, for gods sake! And I bought Ableton last year, after years of working with free (pirated) versions (shh).
I should add I purchased the "light" version as it lay within the range of my purchasing power, and in doing so lost access to much of useful tools I was used to using previously. Makes me sad!
I don't believe that sound engineers and programmers should work for free, but I do agree that making their crafts and tools public is an enabling act which allows anyone to create freely and in turn allows society to produce many, many more great ideas and realisations.

It seems to me like in 1985 it's possible that the kind of future Stallman describes would not have been so far away. It's definately interesting to consider a point in time at which this future would have been only one choice away. The world isn't void of any options when it comes to free software however.
There's always free software for you to use out there, but it's usually of a quality lesser than the major proprietary softwares out there which fill the same function. For example Open Office (which I use) versus Microsoft Office. Some free programs I use work just great for their intended purpose, like processing, p5js, vvvv, paint.net, atom, vlc. I am not sure about the license of all of those, but some are definately open to contribution.

Some major technology companies such as Google or Facebook have grown to gigantic proportions by offering a free-to-use service, and with time have created original business models that allowed them to maintain the bulk of their free services.
This reminds me of Stallman's attemps to portray an economy of the future, though the reality is completely off. While such companies innovate and make their trademark tools available for free, they often destructively in face of competition, and make the creation of cutting-edge tools a race in which the larger competitors are miles ahead of the smaller competitors.

I found this manifesto especially eye-opening because of the perpsective it brings on the debate around the tightening of copyright laws nowadays.
Sure, people should be paid for their work, but I agree that the free dissemination of technology and ideas enables the creation of true wonders, and would likely transform society in a profound manner.
Sure, people could be paid differently for their work (with something else than my personal information please). Is such a transformation possible? Would it ever? Who knows... Wouldn't it be great? Yes!
